I. The scenario

Form your take before you see anyone else's.

Each week, one real product decision from a real company at a real moment in time. Write your unfiltered take across four dimensions — before we show you what shipped and what happened. The point isn't to be right. It's to notice the gap between your instinct and reality, and let that gap teach you.

This week

Meta, in early 2023.

A decision room, not yet decided.

It is early 2023. You are a senior PM at Meta. Twitter has been acquired by Elon Musk and is in active turmoil — verification chaos, advertiser pullback, mass layoffs, and a steady exodus of the cultural-influencer cohort who made the platform sticky.

Mark Zuckerberg's leadership team is debating whether Meta should ship a text-based social app. Internally there is a working prototype built on the Instagram codebase. The Instagram graph is the largest social graph in the Western world (~2B MAU). A text product could in theory bootstrap on it instantly.

Open questions on the table
  • — Tightly couple to Instagram (one-tap signup, IG followers ported in), or stand alone?
  • — What ships at launch? DMs? Hashtags? A chronological feed? A web app?
  • — Is the goal to kill Twitter, complement Instagram, or defend against TikTok absorbing text-social mindshare?
  • — How big is the risk an obvious Twitter-clone gets dismissed by users and press as derivative?

Imagine you're in the room. You don't yet know what they decided or how it played out.

III. What actually happened
The decision they made

Threads.

Launched July 5, 2023.

Shape A tightly-coupled extension of Instagram. One-tap signup with IG credentials. IG followers auto-suggested. Built and shipped by the IG team in roughly five months.

Deliberately omitted No DMs. No hashtags. No trending topics. No web app for several weeks. No chronological feed by default. No following-only feed initially. No way to delete your Threads account without also deleting Instagram.

Mosseri's framing

"Not to replace Twitter — but to build a public square for communities on Instagram that never really embraced it."

News and politics were explicitly de-emphasized. A deliberate aesthetic and policy choice.

The IG-graph hack drove signups but not retention. Account-deletion coupling created immediate backlash; Meta reversed it within months. Features rolled out slowly and deliberately over twelve months — web, following feed, search, hashtags.

It is now widely viewed inside Meta as a successful long-tail bet that traded launch-week retention for durable growth. Threads is widely credited as a meaningful contributor to X's decline.

The tradeoffs worth noticing
Speed-to-market over product polish.

Meta knew the launch product was thin. They optimized for shipping during Twitter's instability window — a market-timing call, not a product-completeness call.

Growth-hack coupling over user trust.

The IG-account lock-in was a deliberate growth lever they expected to reverse. They ate the trust hit on purpose because the alternative was a much slower S-curve.

"Defend" framing, not "kill Twitter" framing.

The actual goal was defensive — keep text-social inside the Meta ecosystem rather than let X or TikTok own it. The Twitter-killer narrative was press, not strategy.

Retention-later over retention-now.

They explicitly tolerated the DAU collapse. The internal metric that mattered was 6- and 12-month retained MAU, not week-1 DAU.
