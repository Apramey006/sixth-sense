This week's scenario

Meta · Early 2023

It is early 2023. You are a senior PM at Meta. Twitter has been acquired by Elon Musk and is in active turmoil — verification chaos, advertiser pullback, mass layoffs, and a steady exodus of the cultural-influencer cohort who made the platform sticky.

Mark Zuckerberg's leadership team is debating whether Meta should ship a text-based social app. Internally there is a working prototype built on the Instagram codebase. The Instagram graph is the largest social graph in the Western world (~2B MAU). A text product could in theory bootstrap on it instantly.

Several open questions are on the table:

  • Should the product be tightly coupled to Instagram (one-tap signup using IG account, IG followers ported in) or stand alone?
  • What features ship at launch? DMs? Hashtags? A chronological feed? A following-only feed? A web app?
  • Is the goal to kill Twitter, complement Instagram, or defend against TikTok absorbing text-social mindshare?
  • How big is the risk that an obvious Twitter-clone gets dismissed by users and press as derivative?

Imagine you're in the room. You don't yet know what they decided or how it played out.

What actually shipped

Threads · launched July 5, 2023

The decision: Ship as a tightly-coupled extension of Instagram. One-tap signup with IG credentials. IG followers auto-suggested. Built and shipped by the IG team in roughly 5 months.

What was deliberately left out at launch: No DMs. No hashtags. No trending topics. No web app for several weeks. No chronological feed by default. No following-only feed initially. No way to delete your Threads account without also deleting your Instagram.

Adam Mosseri's stated framing: not to "replace" Twitter, but to build "a public square for communities on Instagram that never really embraced Twitter." Explicitly de-emphasized news and politics — a deliberate aesthetic and policy choice.

What happened next
  • • 100M signups in 5 days — fastest consumer product launch in history at the time.
  • • DAU collapsed ~80% within 3 weeks. The IG-graph hack drove signups but not retention.
  • • Account-deletion coupling created immediate backlash; Meta reversed it within months.
  • • Slow, deliberate feature rollout over 12 months (web, following feed, search, hashtags).
  • • ~150M MAU by end of 2024, ~100M DAU. Now widely viewed inside Meta as a successful long-tail bet that traded launch-week retention for durable growth.
  • • X (Twitter) lost ~20% of US DAU over the same period; Threads is widely credited as a meaningful contributor.
The interesting tradeoffs to notice
  • Speed-to-market vs. product polish. Meta knew the launch product was thin. They optimized for shipping during Twitter's instability window — a market-timing call, not a product-completeness call.
  • Growth-hack coupling vs. user trust. The IG-account lock-in was a deliberate growth lever that they expected to reverse. They ate the trust hit on purpose because the alternative was a much slower S-curve.
  • "Defend" framing vs. "kill Twitter" framing. The actual goal was defensive — keep text-social inside the Meta ecosystem rather than let X or TikTok own it. The Twitter-killer narrative was press, not strategy.
  • Retention-now vs. retention-later. They explicitly tolerated the DAU collapse. The internal metric that mattered was 6-month and 12-month retained MAU, not week-1 DAU.
